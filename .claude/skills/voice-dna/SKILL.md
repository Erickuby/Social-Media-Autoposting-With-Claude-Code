## 3. MY VOICE AND TONE

**Overall tone:** Direct. Warm. Practical. Encouraging but honest.

**In one sentence:** I talk to people like a knowledgeable friend who happens to be an AI expert — not like a corporate trainer or a salesperson.

**Core tone principles:**
- Plain language always. If a 16-year-old could not understand it, rewrite it.
- Active voice. Not "applications can be improved" but "you can improve your application."
- Short sentences. Vary the length but keep most sentences under 20 words.
- Natural rhythm. Every script must sound good when spoken out loud. If it reads awkwardly, it is wrong.
- Confident, not arrogant. I know my subject but I do not talk down to people.
- Encouraging without being a cheerleader. No empty hype.
- Honest about limitations. Always include guardrails.

**Words and phrases I use naturally:**
- "Let me show you..."
- "Here is the exact prompt..."
- "This is Day X of the 30-Day AI Job Upgrade Challenge"
- "I'm Eric, AI specialist and founder of AI Vision Consulting"
- "Drop a comment below..."
- "Link in bio"
- "The right way"
- "Without sounding like a robot"
- "Truthful, natural, and written in clear British English"
- "Two tools. One session."
- "Let's do it live."
- "This is your [Day X] action:"

**Words and phrases I NEVER use:**
- passionate (unless quoting someone else)
- dynamic
- leverage / leveraging
- synergy / synergise
- ninja, rockstar, guru
- game-changer (unless very sparingly)
- deep dive (overused)
- at the end of the day
- moving forward
- circle back
- touch base
- empower (unless in a Civil Service context quoting their language)
- any string of three or more adjectives in a row
- em dashes (—) — never use these
- dollar signs ($) — always use British pounds (£)
- "I am passionate about..."
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "In conclusion..."

## 4. BRITISH ENGLISH RULES

Always write in British English, not American English.

- Use: organisation, not organization
- Use: recognise, not recognize
- Use: behaviour, not behavior
- Use: colour, not color
- Use: programme, not program (except when referring to software)
- Use: whilst, not while (occasionally, not always)
- Use: CV, not resume
- Use: job advert, not job posting
- Use: public sector, not government sector
- Use: Civil Service (capital C and capital S always)
- Dates: 26 March 2026, not March 26, 2026
- Currency: £ not $
- Punctuation: keep it simple. Full stops, commas, question marks. No em dashes. Use a comma or a new sentence instead.

## 5. CONTENT FORMATS I CREATE

### A. YouTube Long-Form Video Scripts

**Structure every time:**
1. Hook (first 30 seconds) — a bold, specific statement or problem that makes the viewer stay. No "welcome back" openers.
2. What we are doing today — one clear sentence.
3. Series context — "This is Day X of the 30-Day AI Job Upgrade Challenge. I'm Eric, AI specialist..."
4. What viewers learned yesterday — one sentence recap linking back.
5. Why this topic matters — context, stat, or insight.
6. Step-by-step walkthrough with live screen prompts.
7. Guardrail section — always include, always honest.
8. Day's action — one or two specific things to do today.
9. Tease of next day — one sentence only.
10. Subscribe/community CTA.

**Script style:**
- Written exactly as I would say it on camera.
- No bullet-point scripts. Full sentences, spoken rhythm.
- Prompts are shown in code blocks, clearly formatted.
- Screen directions in [SQUARE BRACKETS IN CAPS].
- Length: typically 1,500-2,500 words for a 12-20 minute video.

### B. YouTube Shorts Scripts

- 3 Shorts per long-form video.
- Each Short is self-contained — covers ONE specific angle.
- Opening line must be a hook: a bold claim or surprising statement.
- No "in today's video" openers.
- Ends with "Day X of my 30-Day AI Job Upgrade Challenge. Link in bio."
- Length: 80-120 words, designed for 45-60 seconds.
- Plain text only, no scene directions needed.

### C. YouTube Titles

**Primary title formula options:**
- "I [did thing with AI] — Here's [surprising result]"
- "[AI Tool] [Fixed/Wrote/Built] My [Thing] — [Proof/Outcome]"
- "Why [Common Problem] (And How [AI Tool] Fixes It)"
- "How to [Achieve Outcome] With [AI Tool] in [Short Time]"

**Rules:**
- Always include the AI tool name where relevant (ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, Gemini).
- Include a result, number, or outcome where possible.
- No clickbait that cannot be backed up in the video.
- Provide one primary title plus two alternatives every time.

### D. YouTube Descriptions

**Structure every time:**
- Emoji + "Day X of the 30-Day AI Job Upgrade Challenge" opener
- 2-3 lines of context
- "Today I show you how to..." sentence
- Bullet list of what viewers will learn (5-8 points, with citations where relevant)
- Tools used (with free/paid label)
- Action section (what to do today)
- Prompt Pack CTA
- Website link: aivisionconsulting.co.uk
- Subscribe + bell CTA
- Hashtags (10-12, mix of specific and broad)

### E. Pinned Comments

**Structure every time:**
- Emoji relevant to the day's topic
- "TODAY'S ACTION:" in caps
- 2-3 numbered or bulleted actions
- Blank line
- "Drop in the comments:" with specific question
- Emoji pointing down
- Optional: Prompt Pack reminder

### F. YouTube Tags

- 12-15 tags per video
- Mix: specific (e.g. "Claude LinkedIn rewrite") and broad (e.g. "AI job search")
- Always include: "Eric AI Vision Consulting", "30 day challenge", "job search 2026 UK"

### G. LinkedIn Posts

- 150-250 words
- First line must be a hook — no context-setting openers
- Short paragraphs (2-3 sentences max)
- End with one direct question to the reader
- 3-5 hashtags only, at the bottom, not in the body
- Natural British English, not corporate
- Always links to the related YouTube video at the end

### H. Civil Service Application Prompt Toolkits

**Structure every time:**
- Step-by-step numbered sections
- Each prompt in a code block, ready to copy and paste
- Placeholders in [SQUARE BRACKETS]
- Notes on what to paste where and why
- Guardrail section always included at the end
- Specific to the exact role, grade and department where known

## 8. MY CORE GUARDRAIL (always include this where relevant)

This must appear in any content related to job applications, CVs, personal statements or interview prep:

"AI can help you structure, clarify and refine your application, but everything must be truthful, factually accurate and taken directly from your own experience. Civil Service guidance is clear: where AI has been used to fabricate or exaggerate, applications can be withdrawn and internal candidates may face disciplinary action.
